How To Write A Novel

Taking you from idea to reality!

Have you ever wanted to write a novel but didn't know where to start? Well look no further, for this page will give you a complete understanding of how to get started with getting your awesome ideas from your mind onto the paper, from brainstorming to publishing.

Step 1: Brainstorm

The very first step in developing your novel is brainstorming. Some would say that this is the easiest step, but others would disagree. Brainstorming can be hard because there are literally infinite different ideas, plots, and characters just floating in space, waiting for you to pluck them out of the air. So, in order to not become super overwhelmed, I'd reccommend doing one or more of these different brainstorming exercises:

  • Mind Map

    • This is an exercise for pouring out your thoughts in a semi-organised format. You can put a working book title in a bubble in the middle of your page, then just draw lines coming out of the bubble and add bubbles to the end of those lines, and lines and bubbles to those ideas, and so on. This is just to get your creative juices going, and to show you that one idea can lead to another, and that one to another...

  • Sticky Note Wall

    • For this technique, you might need to go to the nearest supply store and buy sticky notes in bulk. When you have them, go to an available wall in your house and activate your creative juices. As soon as an idea or thought occurs to you, no matter how incoherent or non-relevant, write it down on a sticky note and stick it to the wall. Then, repeat.

  • Freewriting

    • This one is a personal favorite, and it involves...writing. Just sit down, and write a story. Any story, about anything, and before you know it, you'll be having creative novel ideas galore.

  • Writing Prompts

    • I also do this occasionaly. Writing prompts are just ideas, thought up by someone else, that are read to get the juices going in your creative subconcious. You can find thousands of these sites online and hundreds books with these. I would reccommend the prompts by Bryn Donovan.

    Step 2 (Optional): Write An Outline

    This is optional, and depends on whether you are a Pantser or a Plotter. A Pantser is a writer that doesn't like to plan their plot; they just sit down and write by the seat of their pants. A plotter, on the other hand, prefers to write a plan, called an Outline, of their novel, so that they can have a clear sense of the road ahead, and in order to find any plot holes and eradicate them beforehand. Again, this is completely up to you. An example of a chapter outline for a novel could be:

    1. Joe wakes up and is confused by his surroundings, which look sleek and futuristic, unlike his dirty and cluttered apartment he lives in.
    2. The door opens and a three-headed creature carrying a tray of strange food walks in, smiling at Joe with all six of it's mouths.
    3. Joe screams and tries to leave the room, but the door closes automatically, closing him in with the strange creature.
    4. Joe demands answers, and the alien explains that they are from the planet Groth, and they need a human to bring back to save their planet.
    5. Joe refuses, but after being told of how terrible life has been on Groth, and why they need Joe so much, Joe reluctantly agrees to go along with them. For now.
    6. End of Chapter Six

    Step 3: Begin Writing

    This is where the fun begins. At this point in the process, its all up to you. You can write your novel longhand and then type it onto a computer, or you can do it right onto the computer. I personally have an electronic Brother AX-28 typewriter that I use for my first draft, and then I compile my second draft on the computer. You can do whatever you want.

    Step 4: Edit

    This is where the stuff that's not that fun begins. Editing is the process of checking through your novel, correcting spelling mistakes, plot holes, grammar mistakes, and anything that makes your work of art less than a work of art. If you're aiming for traditional publishing and want to go for the best novel you can, you can pay one of many different types of proffesional editors to edit your manuscript for you, or for the less but not worthless strategy, you can do it yourself.

    Step 5: Prepare A Cover Image

    This is where it can get fun again. This is the point where you just finished your manuscript, and now you are ready to show it to the world. But first, you need an amazing book cover, because despite what most people say, people do judge a book by it's cover, so make sure yours looks proffesional. I'd obviously reccommend getting your cover done by a proffesional cover artist, on a site like Fiverr, which allows people to buy proffesional covers from freelance artists for $5-300. But if that just doesn't fit into your budget, a free self-website designer like Adobe Spark or Canvas works too.

    Step 6: Publish

    You did it! You now have a perfect manuscript, polished and clothed, and you are ready to show the world your story (and maybe make a little bit of money off of it too). You know have three options. One, keep the book un-published. If you don't really want to reveal your baby to the world, and just want to hand out your novel to a couple of friends and family members, that is perfectly fine. The second option is traditional publishing. This is where you send your manuscript off to a publisher and if they read it and like it, they will publish it, and own the rights to it. But just a heads up: Don't be crushed if you receive a letter back from a publishing rejecting you. Less than 1% of all unsolicited manuscripts sent to publishers are rejected, so it is incredibly hard to be published this way, which is why more and more people are leaning to the third option. Self-Publishing. Self-Publishing is when you own all of the rights to your book, and you have complete freedom with how you present it, and through major self-publishers like Amazon KDP, Draft2Digital and Smashwords, you can get up to over 80% of the money. Pretty good, right? You can choose how much to price your book for, how long to keep it like that, and much more. The only downsides are that in traditional publishing, the higher ups take care of most of the work, while you just sit back and get money and go to author signings. But tradtional publishing has a lot of flaws too, but I won't go into that right now.

    Conclusion

    Congratulations! You did it. I hope you make millions of dollars, and that you can't walk ten feet from your mansion without being mobbed for autographs and selfies.
